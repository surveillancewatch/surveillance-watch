SeaGlass: Detecting Cell-Site Simulators in the Puget Sound

How UW researchers used $500 sensors in rideshare vehicles to find suspicious cellular activity near a federal immigration facility and Sea-Tac Airport — and the tools civilians can use to detect surveillance today.

Published May 11, 2026 WA Surveillance Watch Research Team Open Source / Public Domain
Source Methodology: This report is derived from the published academic paper, university press releases, vetted journalism, EFF publications, and public project repositories. No non-public information was used. All sources are cited inline and compiled in Appendix A.
Related: Cell-Site Simulator Surveillance in Tacoma, Washington
Full report on the DHS-funded StingRay program that operated secretly from 2008–2014 in the same region SeaGlass was scanning.

Executive Summary

In 2016–2017, University of Washington researchers deployed a fleet of low-cost sensors in rideshare vehicles across Seattle and Milwaukee to detect cell-site simulators (IMSI catchers) — the same surveillance technology Tacoma PD secretly operated just 30 miles south. The SeaGlass project proved that city-scale surveillance detection was feasible with $500 in off-the-shelf hardware, and found anomalies consistent with IMSI catcher activity near a DHS immigration services facility in Tukwila and Seattle-Tacoma International Airport.

Though SeaGlass itself is now dormant, it spawned a lineage of increasingly accessible detection tools. Today, the EFF’s RayHunter lets anyone detect cell-site simulators with a $20 mobile hotspot. This report covers the original research, its findings, and the current state of civilian IMSI catcher detection.

1. The SeaGlass Project

“SeaGlass: Enabling City-Wide IMSI-Catcher Detection” was published in the Proceedings on Privacy Enhancing Technologies (PoPETs), Volume 2017, Issue 3. The paper was authored by:

  • Peter Ney — Ph.D. student (co-lead; later moved to Google)
  • Ian Smith — Research Scientist (co-lead; later moved to Google)
  • Gabriel Cadamuro — Ph.D. student (statistical modeling)
  • Tadayoshi Kohno — Professor, faculty PI (left UW in 2026 for Georgetown University)

All four were at the UW Allen School of Computer Science & Engineering, affiliated with the Security and Privacy Research Lab and the UW Tech Policy Lab.[1][2]

Funding: $35,000 Knight Foundation Prototype Fund grant (Knight News Challenge on Data, February 2016) and the Short-Dooley Professorship at UW.[3]

Deployment: March–May 2016, 15 rideshare vehicles (9 Seattle, 6 Milwaukee), ~2 months, drivers paid $25/week. Collected 2.1 million cellular scans, mapped 1,411 towers in Seattle and 609 in Milwaukee.[1]

2. Sensor Architecture

Each sensor cost $502 in off-the-shelf parts — vs. $100,000+ for a commercial cell surveyor (QRC Surveyor 500). A 10-sensor fleet: ~$6,220/year including data.[1]

Sensor Bill of Materials ($502/unit)

Telit GT-864 GSM modem$65
External antenna$25
Raspberry Pi 2B+$35
GPS (GlobalSat BU-353)$30
Bait phone (Moto-G + SnoopSnitch)$95
4G hotspot + 3-month data$100
DC/AC inverter$26
Hub, cables, SD card, enclosure$126

How it worked: The GSM modem scanned every 10 seconds, recording all base station frequencies, signal strengths, bit error rates, and 22+ BCCH properties at each GPS location. The Pi cached data locally and uploaded to a cloud database. The bait phone ran SnoopSnitch to independently log suspicious events like cipher downgrades and IMSI requests. Units packed into vehicle trunks with antenna near windows; auto-boot on ignition.[1]

GSM only by design: While 4G IMSI catchers existed (Harris Hailstorm), GSM simulators were more prevalent and could attack 3G/4G phones via downgrade attacks. The architecture is generalizable to 3G/4G with appropriate modems.[1]

3. Detection Methodology

Five signatures, five detection methods:[1]

SignatureDetection MethodWhat It Catches
Multi-location transmissionBase station modeling (log-distance path loss)Portable IMSI catchers moving while operating
ImpermanenceTemporal filtering (7-day window)Devices that appear briefly then power off
Anomalous configurationBCCH outlier detection (22 properties)Unusual broadcast parameters not matching carrier norms
Geographic inconsistencyChannel and LAC analysisFrequencies or location codes mismatching local carrier
Suspicious phone interactionBait phone logs (SnoopSnitch)Cipher downgrades, IMSI requests, silent SMS

Validation: 100 synthetic CSS measurements injected at varying distances. At 4 km: ~80% recall, ~70% precision. Below 1 km: near-zero recall (CSS hides in legitimate tower’s signal envelope).[1]

4. What They Found

Two compelling anomalies in greater Seattle:[1][2]

Anomaly 1: Near USCIS Building, Tukwila

A tower near the USCIS (DHS immigration services) building transmitted on six different frequencies over two months:

  • 96% of all towers broadcast on a single channel; remaining 4% used 2–3
  • Five of six frequencies detectable only within ~400 meters of the building
  • Primary channel measurable across 5 km

Consistent with a stationary IMSI catcher operating from inside the building.[1]

Anomaly 2: Sea-Tac Airport

A single transient measurement with four simultaneously anomalous broadcast properties:

  • MSTXPWR = 7 (normal: 0–5) — controls max phone transmit power
  • T3212 = 66 (normal: 9–10) — forces far more frequent location updates
  • RXACCMIN = 18 (normal: 0–12) — minimum signal level for cell access
  • CRH = 1 (normal: 2–4) — cell reselection hysteresis

Same tower showed normal properties before and after — consistent with an intermittent CSS.[1]

Researcher caution: “Without access to ground truth, we cannot definitively classify these anomalies as cell-site simulators. That would require additional corroborating evidence.”[1]

5. Retroactive Validation

The Tukwila anomaly gained significance as ICE’s CSS use was subsequently documented:

  • ACLU FOIA (2019): ICE deployed CSS at least 466 times (2017–2019), with 223 by September 2017 — the same period SeaGlass was scanning[6]
  • BuzzFeed: 1,885+ ICE CSS deployments (2013–2017)[7]
  • DHS Inspector General (2023): ICE agents “repeatedly disregarded the law requiring a warrant”[8]
  • ICE (May 2025): Spent $825,000 on CSS-equipped surveillance vehicles[9]
  • Portland (Oct 2025): Protesters near ICE facility reported fake cell tower signals[10]

Key context: SeaGlass detected its anomaly near the Tukwila USCIS building during the same period ICE was later confirmed to have been deploying CSS hundreds of times nationally. The researchers couldn’t prove it — but subsequent evidence strongly suggests they were right.

EFF RayHunter: The Current Standard

Released March 2025, RayHunter runs on cheap hotspots (primarily the Orbic RC400L, ~$20) and intercepts control-plane traffic in real time.[11]

Detects: 2G downgrades (tower forces device from 4G to 2G), null ciphers (encryption disabled), and IMSI grabs (tower requests permanent identity without authentication).

Field results (Sep 2025): Validated against commercial CSS — detected every attack. No CSS at US protests. Suspicious activity at 2024 DNC in Chicago (devices near delegate hotel switched to new tower, IMSI requested, then disconnected). Thousands of devices deployed; dozens of captures submitted.[11][12]

FADe Project: SeaGlass Goes Global

The most direct descendant. Managed by South Lighthouse, funded by Open Technology Fund, with direct technical assistance from the UW SeaGlass team. Deployed in 9 Latin American countries, scanned ~9,000 antennas, found 150+ likely IMSI catchers.[13]

Marlin: The Academic Frontier

NDSS 2025. Identified 53 messages an IMSI catcher must send to extract identities. 99.9% detection rate, first statistically significant evidence (p << 0.005) at a public event. Approach is “causal, not correlated” — detects what CSS must do, not just what they might look like.[14]

For Enterprises

  • ESD Overwatch / CryptoPhone — strategic real-time detection; enterprise pricing
  • GSMK CryptoPhone CP700 — built-in baseband firewall (~$3,500/unit)
  • Bastille Networks — facility RF monitoring, 100 kHz–6 GHz

Fundamental limitation: All tools detect only active IMSI catchers that transmit signals. Passive IMSI catchers that only listen are undetectable because they emit nothing.

9. Pending Legislation

Federal: Cell-Site Simulator Warrant Act of 2025

H.R.4811 / S.2522 (119th Congress, bipartisan):[15]

  • Require warrants for all government CSS use
  • Limit operation to 30 days
  • Require FCC-certified testing lab certification
  • Mandate disclosure at constitutionally protected gatherings

Washington State

HB 1440 (signed May 11, 2015) requires probable cause warrants, data deletion for bystanders, and judicial education. Full details in our Tacoma report.

Seattle Surveillance Ordinance

SMC 14.18 (July 2017) requires council approval before any city department acquires surveillance technology. The ACLU-WA called it “the nation’s strongest.”[16]

10. What You Can Do

Detect: Install EFF RayHunter on a supported hotspot ($20–30). Submit any detections to EFF for analysis.

Request records: File public records requests with your local police asking about CSS acquisition, use, FBI NDAs, and Harris Corporation contracts. WA Surveillance Watch provides free templates.

Support legislation: Urge representatives to support the Cell-Site Simulator Warrant Act (H.R.4811 / S.2522). Push for compliance audits of WA’s HB 1440.

Monitor: Carry a RayHunter hotspot at public demonstrations. Continued monitoring builds the evidence base.
