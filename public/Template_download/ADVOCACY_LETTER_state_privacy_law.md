## WASHINGTON'S SURVEILLANCE LANDSCAPE

Washington has been at the forefront of both surveillance deployment and privacy protection, but significant gaps remain:

**What we know:**
- Over **80 Washington cities** currently use automated license plate reader (ALPR) networks that photograph every vehicle that passes, building a searchable database of residents' movements
- A **University of Washington Center for Human Rights report** (October 2025) documented that at least 18 Washington law enforcement agencies had unauthorized federal access to local surveillance data, with 8 agencies directly sharing data with Border Patrol
- In November 2025, a **Skagit County Superior Court** ruled in *Rodriguez v. City of Sedro Woolley* that ALPR data is public record under the Public Records Act — but it took a lawsuit to establish this
- Law enforcement agencies are increasingly purchasing personal data from **commercial data brokers** like Palantir, Babel Street, and Fog Data Science — circumventing warrant requirements

**What Washington has done well:**
- **Shield Law (RCW 10.93.160)** — Prohibits state/local agencies from using resources for immigration enforcement
- **Facial Recognition Accountability (SB 6280)** — Requires accountability reports, bias testing, and meaningful human review
- **Privacy Act (RCW 9.73)** — Strong warrant requirements for electronic surveillance
- **State Constitution, Article I, Section 7** — Broader privacy protections than the federal Fourth Amendment

**What's still missing:**
- No requirement for **public notice or approval** before agencies deploy new surveillance technologies
- No **comprehensive inventory** requirement — residents often have no way to know what surveillance their city uses
- No restriction on **purchasing personal data from brokers** without a warrant
- No mandatory **data retention limits** for surveillance camera systems
- Inadequate enforcement mechanisms for existing privacy protections

## WHAT I'M ASKING

I urge you to introduce or support legislation that:

### 1. Community Control of Surveillance Technology
Require city councils and county commissions to **publicly approve** the acquisition of surveillance technology before deployment, including:
- A public impact assessment
- A community comment period
- A recorded vote
- Annual reporting on use and effectiveness

*Model: Seattle's Surveillance Ordinance (SMC 14.18) and similar laws in Oakland, CA and Cambridge, MA*

### 2. Close the Data Broker Loophole
Prohibit state and local agencies from **purchasing personal data** (including location data, social media data, and communication metadata) from commercial data brokers without a warrant, applying the same standard required for direct collection.

### 3. Mandatory Surveillance Audits
Require the **Washington State Auditor** or Attorney General to conduct periodic audits of law enforcement surveillance technology use, including:
- Compliance with the Shield Law
- Compliance with the Facial Recognition Accountability Act
- Data sharing with federal agencies
- Data retention and deletion practices

### 4. Strengthen the Shield Law
Expand RCW 10.93.160 to explicitly cover:
- Data broker purchases used for immigration enforcement purposes
- Indirect data sharing (e.g., sharing via multi-agency databases accessible to federal agencies)
- Clear penalties for non-compliance

### 5. Data Retention Limits
Establish **maximum retention periods** for surveillance data collected about people not suspected of a crime, particularly ALPR data and facial recognition probe images.

## WHY THIS MATTERS NOW

The November 2025 court ruling in *Rodriguez v. Sedro Woolley* was a significant victory for transparency, but it shouldn't require a lawsuit for residents to learn what surveillance their government uses. Proactive legislation is needed.

The UW Human Rights report showed that existing laws are not being consistently followed. Oversight without enforcement is merely suggestion.

Washington has historically led the nation on privacy rights. I'm asking you to continue that tradition by ensuring that surveillance technology — however useful — is deployed with transparency, accountability, and respect for civil liberties.
