Cell-Site Simulator Surveillance in Tacoma, Washington

How a DHS-funded StingRay program operated in secret for six years, shielded by FBI non-disclosure agreements and misleading court orders.

Published May 11, 2026 WA Surveillance Watch Research Team Open Source / Public Domain
Source Methodology: Every claim in this report is derived from publicly available sources including court filings, government records, FOIA/PRA responses, vetted journalism, and civil liberties organization publications. No non-public or privileged information was used. All sources are cited inline and compiled in Appendix A.

Executive Summary

Between 2008 and 2014, the Tacoma Police Department secretly operated cell-site simulator surveillance technology—commonly known as a “StingRay”—funded by federal homeland security grants and shielded from public disclosure by a non-disclosure agreement with the FBI. The program was justified to city officials as counter-terrorism equipment but was used almost exclusively for drug investigations. When the program became public in August 2014, it triggered public records lawsuits, over $361,000 in court-ordered penalties against the City of Tacoma, and directly prompted Washington State to pass one of the nation’s strongest cell-site simulator warrant laws.

This report compiles the complete public record of Tacoma’s cell-site simulator program from primary sources.

1. What is a Cell-Site Simulator?

A cell-site simulator (CSS), also marketed under the brand names StingRay, KingFish, and Hailstorm, is a device manufactured by Harris Corporation (now L3Harris Technologies) that mimics a legitimate cell tower. When activated, all cell phones in the vicinity connect to the simulator instead of a real tower, allowing the operator to:

  • Identify the location of a target phone
  • Collect identifying information (IMSI/IMEI numbers) from every phone in range
  • In some configurations, intercept call and text metadata

The technology was originally developed for military and intelligence use. Beginning in the mid-2000s, Harris Corporation began selling versions to state and local law enforcement, with FBI approval required for each sale.[1]

2. Acquisition Timeline

2007–2008: Initial Purchase

The Tacoma Police Department first acquired cell-site simulator technology in 2007–2008. According to records obtained through public records requests:

  • TPD Detective Jeffrey Shipp procured a $450,000 training and equipment grant for a “cellular phone tracking system”—one of only five such grants awarded nationally. Shipp was named TPD Employee of the Month for securing the grant.[2][3]
  • In 2008, the federal government shipped a StingRay and a KingFish device to Detective Shipp via FedEx overnight.[3]
  • The grant originated from the Department of Justice through the Counterdrug Technology Assessment Center, Technology Transfer Program—a federal program providing equipment to local law enforcement for drug enforcement.[3]
  • A 2007 internal memo sought to bypass competitive bidding procedures, citing Harris Corporation as the sole manufacturer.[2]

March 2013: Major Upgrade

On March 19, 2013, the Tacoma City Council unanimously approved a $251,752.41 equipment upgrade:

  • $188,814 (75%) came from a DHS Port Security Grant
  • $62,938 (25%) came from the Tacoma Police Federal Asset Seizure Fund (local match)
  • The upgrade included a StingRay II Enhancement and a Hailstorm device (4G LTE-capable) at $109,421 (GSA contract pricing)
  • A $3,500 laptop was bundled into the purchase price[4][5][10]

Police Chief Donald Ramsdell and Detective Shipp presented the upgrade to the City Council’s Board of Contracts and Awards as supporting the Explosives Ordinance Detail (EOD) with “IED prevention, protection, response and recovery measures.”[5][10]

City Council members later stated they did not understand the full capabilities of the technology when they approved the purchase.[5]

February 2014: Additional Software

TPD purchased $51,727 in software from Verint Systems (Melville, NY), a company founded by former Israeli intelligence operatives. The purchase is believed to be the SkyLock cell-phone location-tracking system.[6]

3. Federal Funding and Grant Programs

The acquisition of Tacoma’s cell-site simulator technology was funded through multiple federal grant programs, bypassing normal local appropriations and oversight:

Tacoma sits within the Seattle Urban Area Security Initiative (UASI) region, which encompasses King, Pierce, and Snohomish Counties. UASI grants have been used throughout the Puget Sound region to fund law enforcement technology with minimal local legislative scrutiny, since the grants do not require cities to appropriate their own funds.[5][10]

Key finding: The terrorism and IED justification used to secure both the original grant and the 2013 upgrade was acknowledged as pretextual. TPD later admitted the cell-site simulator was never used for IED-related purposes.[7][10]

4. The FBI Non-Disclosure Agreement

As a condition of purchasing Harris Corporation equipment, the Tacoma Police Department was required to sign a non-disclosure agreement with the FBI.

Authorization Chain

On February 13, 2013, FBI Technical Surveillance Section Chief W. L. Scott Bean III sent a letter to Harris Corporation (addressed to Patricia Sciandra) authorizing the sale of “the state-and-local version of the Stingray product with the restricted Landshark software” to TPD. This authorization referenced:

  • Contract J-FBI-09-211 (the master FBI–Harris Corporation agreement)
  • A separate April 6, 2010 agreement between FBI and Harris Corporation
  • An existing NDA between the FBI and TPD

A PDF of this letter was released by the Center for Effective Humane Rights Protection (CEHRP) on May 19, 2015.[8]

NDA Terms

The NDA, signed on January 3, 2013 by Police Chief Ramsdell, FBI Special Agent Laura Laughlin (Seattle Field Office), and four TPD officers, contained provisions that:

  • Required TPD to notify the FBI before responding to any public records request about the device
  • Required TPD to allow the FBI “sufficient time to seek to prevent disclosure”
  • Prohibited use of information about the device in any civil or criminal proceeding without prior written FBI approval
  • Included a “drop prosecution” clause: the FBI could compel TPD to dismiss criminal cases rather than reveal stingray use in court[8][9][10]

Nationwide Pattern

The Tacoma NDA followed a template used by at least 23 agencies across the country, including departments in San Diego, Chicago, Miami, Baltimore, and Tucson. Harris Corporation was required to notify the FBI whenever any local department sought to purchase a StingRay, and the FBI imposed the NDA as a precondition.[9][17]

5. Operational Use: 2009–2014

Deployment Statistics

Public records obtained by journalists and through litigation revealed:

  • Between April 2009 and June 2014, TPD deployed the cell-site simulator at least 179 times, each with a court order[7][10]
  • An additional 10 emergency deployments occurred without prior judicial approval[3]
  • In the first half of 2014 alone, drug-case deployments exceeded the total number of all deployments across the previous five years combined[7]

Misrepresentation to Courts

TPD obtained pen register / trap and trace (PR/TT) court orders from Pierce County Superior Court rather than full search warrants. This is significant because:

  • PR/TT orders require only a showing that information is “relevant and material” to an investigation—a lower standard than probable cause[7]
  • The applications described the technology as a “cellular tracking device” without explaining it was a cell-site simulator or describing its capabilities[3][13]
  • Pierce County Superior Court judges did not know they were authorizing stingray surveillance[13]
  • A TPD detective stated under oath that officers intentionally omitted any mention of cell-site simulators from case reports[3]
  • Neither prosecutors nor defense attorneys were informed of stingray use[13]

Shared Use With Other Agencies

TPD also deployed its cell-site simulator on behalf of other agencies, including:

  • Pierce County Sheriff’s Department
  • DEA Seattle Field Division
  • U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE)[3][13]

6. Public Disclosure: August 2014

The Tacoma News Tribune broke the story in August 2014 after reporter Adam Lynn obtained operational deployment data through public records requests.[13][14]

Key revelations that followed:

  • Pierce County Superior Court judges expressed surprise at the scope of surveillance they had unknowingly authorized
  • City Council members said they were not told the device’s full capabilities
  • TPD acknowledged the device had never been used for its stated IED/counter-terrorism purpose[7]
  • Privacy activist Phil Mocek filed a public records request for the FBI NDA in mid-2014, which TPD heavily redacted despite identical NDAs from other agencies already being publicly available[9][15]

After the media exposure, Pierce County Superior Court judges in November 2014 ordered TPD to:

  • Specify device usage in all future court order applications
  • Refrain from retaining data collected on non-suspects[13]

7. Public Records Litigation

Mocek v. City of Tacoma (2015–2017)

Filed: Fall 2015, Pierce County Superior Court

Privacy activist Phil Mocek and the Center for Open Policing sued over TPD’s redaction of the FBI NDA. When released, the document had been redacted to show only the title page, introductory paragraph, and signatures—“the middle was a solid wall of black ink”—despite unredacted copies of identical NDAs from other agencies already being in public circulation.[9][15]

Ruling: Judge Frank Cuthbertson found the redactions were not “essential to effective law enforcement.” The court imposed a $100/day penalty for 500 days of wrongful withholding (June 21, 2014 through November 3, 2015).

Cost to City of Tacoma: $50,000 plus legal fees.[12][15]

Banks/Christopher v. City of Tacoma (2016–2021)

Filed: February 11, 2016, Pierce County Superior Court

The ACLU of Washington filed suit on behalf of four Tacoma residents:

  • Rev. Gregory Christopher—President, Tacoma NAACP chapter
  • Rev. Arthur Banks—Black church leader
  • Rev. Toney Montgomery—President, Tacoma Ministerial Alliance
  • Whitney Brady—2015 City Council candidate

Represented by ACLU-WA staff attorneys La Rond Baker and Jared Friend, and cooperating attorneys from Schwabe Williamson & Wyatt.[4][11]

Trial ruling (2018): Judge Helen G. Whitener (later elevated to the Washington State Supreme Court) found the City of Tacoma had violated the Public Records Act and failed to produce 11 documents that should have been disclosed.[11]

Appeal (June 2, 2021): Division II of the WA Court of Appeals (Judges Glasgow, Maxa, Sutton) upheld penalties of $182,340. Three issues were remanded for further proceedings.[11]

Final resolution: City and plaintiffs settled remaining issues for an additional $75,000, plus $115,530 in attorney fees.

Total cost to City of Tacoma: $311,607[4][11]

Broader Pattern

The City of Tacoma accumulated approximately $3 million in Public Records Act penalties across all cases over a five-year period (2014–2019).[11]

8. Legislative Response: HB 1440

The Tacoma stingray revelations directly inspired the Washington State Legislature to act.

ESHB 1440 — Cell-Site Simulator Warrant Requirements

  • Introduced: January 21, 2015, by Rep. David Taylor (R-Moxee)
  • Co-sponsors: Representatives Goodman, Pollet, Scott, Condotta, Shea, G. Hunt, Young, Moscoso, Smith, Ryu, Jinkins, Magendanz, Farrell, McCaslin
  • House vote: March 4, 2015—97–0 (unanimous)
  • Senate vote: April 14, 2015—47–0 (unanimous)
  • Signed by Governor Jay Inslee: May 11, 2015[3][16]

Key Provisions (codified at RCW 9.73)

  1. Law enforcement must demonstrate probable cause before deploying a cell-site simulator
  2. Court orders valid for a maximum of 60 days, with extensions requiring new applications
  3. Data deletion mandate: Information collected on non-target individuals must not be used and must be deleted promptly
  4. Judicial education requirements ensuring judges understand the technology (first of their kind nationally)
  5. Emergency exceptions for exigent circumstances[3][16]

The ACLU called this law one that made Washington “a leader in restricting use of invasive Stingrays.” Washington’s data-deletion provisions were the first of their kind nationally and influenced subsequent Department of Justice policy.[3][16]

Note: May 11, 2026, marks the 11th anniversary of Governor Inslee signing HB 1440 into law.

9. Related: SeaGlass IMSI-Catcher Detection

In 2017, University of Washington researchers led by Professor Tadayoshi Kohno deployed the SeaGlass system—sensors installed in 15 ridesharing vehicles that scanned the cellular spectrum across Seattle and Tacoma over two months.[18]

Key findings:

  • Measured 1,400+ distinct cell towers in the Seattle–Tacoma area
  • Detected dozens of anomalies consistent with IMSI-catcher activity
  • An anomalous tower near a USCIS (immigration services) building south of Seattle was broadcasting on six different frequencies—96% of all legitimate towers use only one to three
  • A suspicious signal near Seattle-Tacoma International Airport exhibited broadcast properties “well outside the range expected for any tower in the city”

Researchers cautioned that definitive confirmation of IMSI-catcher activity would require corroborating evidence.[18]

10. Where in Washington is This Technology?

Cell-site simulator use in Washington State extends well beyond Tacoma. Based on public records, court filings, academic research, and the EFF Atlas of Surveillance, here is what is known about CSS presence across the state.

State and Local Agencies

AgencyStatusEvidence
Tacoma Police Dept. Confirmed StingRay, KingFish (2008), Hailstorm (2013), Verint SkyLock (2014). 179+ documented deployments. EFF Atlas of Surveillance entry.[19]
Pierce County Sheriff Suspected No independent possession confirmed, but TPD deployed its StingRay on behalf of Pierce County Sheriff on multiple occasions.[3][13]
Seattle Police Dept. Unacknowledged SPD returned no responsive records to MuckRock FOIA requests for CSS information. However, UW SeaGlass detected anomalies consistent with IMSI-catcher activity near USCIS and Sea-Tac Airport within Seattle’s jurisdiction.[18]
Washington State Patrol Unknown No public records, disclosures, or EFF Atlas entries confirm or deny CSS possession.
King County Sheriff Unknown No public disclosure. King County falls within the Seattle UASI region, which has received DHS grant funding used for surveillance equipment elsewhere.

Federal Agencies Operating in Washington

AgencyStatusEvidence
FBI Seattle Field Office Confirmed FBI administered the NDA program for all Harris Corporation sales. FBI Seattle (SA Laura Laughlin) directly supervised the Tacoma PD NDA. FBI is the largest known domestic CSS operator nationally.[8][17]
DEA Seattle Field Division Confirmed TPD deployed its StingRay on behalf of DEA Seattle. DEA is confirmed nationally as one of the heaviest CSS users, spending over $71M on the technology (DOJ-wide) from 2010–2014.[3][13]
ICE / HSI Confirmed TPD deployed its StingRay on behalf of ICE. DHS (ICE’s parent agency) spent over $24M on CSS technology nationally (2010–2014). SeaGlass detected anomalies near a USCIS building south of Seattle.[3][18]
U.S. Marshals Service Suspected USMS operates its own CSS fleet nationally and is known to lend devices to local agencies. No WA-specific public records, but USMS maintains a fugitive task force in Seattle.
CBP / Border Patrol Unknown CBP has acknowledged CSS use at the national level. CBP operates at the WA–Canada border and at Sea-Tac Airport. No WA-specific disclosures.

Anomalous Signal Detections (SeaGlass 2017)

The University of Washington’s SeaGlass project detected the following anomalies consistent with IMSI-catcher activity in the Seattle–Tacoma corridor:[18]

LocationAnomalyPossible Operator
Near USCIS building, south of Seattle Tower broadcasting on 6 frequencies (96% of towers use 1–3) DHS / ICE immigration enforcement
Near Seattle-Tacoma International Airport Broadcast properties “well outside the range expected for any tower in the city” CBP, TSA, or airport security operations

Transparency gap: Tacoma remains the only Washington agency with extensively documented CSS possession and use—not because it is the only user, but because it is the only agency whose program was exposed through investigative journalism and public records litigation. The FBI NDA framework actively suppresses disclosure by all participating agencies.[9][17]

What You Can Do

Washington’s Public Records Act (RCW 42.56) gives every resident the right to request records about surveillance technology from any state or local agency. WA Surveillance Watch provides free templates for filing cell-site simulator records requests, including templates specific to CSS acquisition documents, usage logs, and FBI NDA agreements.

12. Current Status and Open Questions

  1. Does TPD still operate cell-site simulators? A 2022 MuckRock public records request sought updated information about TPD’s current CSS inventory, including newer technologies from Octasic/Nyxcell and DRT. The response status is unknown.[9]
  2. What other Washington agencies possess cell-site simulators? The EFF Atlas of Surveillance confirms Tacoma PD’s StingRay acquisition. Seattle PD has not acknowledged possession, but SeaGlass detected anomalies consistent with IMSI-catcher activity in the Seattle area. Washington State Patrol’s status is unknown.[18][19]
  3. Are HB 1440’s warrant requirements being followed? No public audit of compliance has been conducted since the law took effect.
  4. What data was collected on bystanders? During six years of warrantless operation (2008–2014), every deployment collected identifying information from all phones in the device’s range. The scope of incidental collection and whether that data was retained or destroyed is unknown.
