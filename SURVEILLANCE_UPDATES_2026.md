## 1. OLYMPIA CITY COUNCIL - FLOCK SAFETY SUSPENSION

### Decision: Program Suspended (December 2025)

On **December 2, 2025**, the Olympia City Council voted to suspend the city's Flock Safety Camera Pilot Program in response to community concerns about data collection, access, and privacy protections.

### Key Details

**Program Scope**:
- **15 cameras** installed at 6 intersections
- **Contract period**: March 2024 - July 2026 (2 years)
- **Total cost**: $98,550 (funded by Washington State Department of Commerce grant)
- **Data volume**: ~4 million images captured per month

**Program Effectiveness**:
According to Interim Police Chief Shelby Parker:
- **112 arrests** aided by Flock system
- **29 stolen vehicles** recovered
- **1 kidnapping** case supported
- **6 homicides** investigations supported

### Reasons for Suspension

Interim Police Chief Shelby Parker recommended suspension citing:

1. **Community Trust**: Importance of maintaining public confidence
2. **Privacy Risks**: Need to reassess potential privacy impacts
3. **Data Access Concerns**: Federal immigration enforcement access issues
4. **Sanctuary City Status**: Protecting Olympia's sanctuary city policies

### Federal Data Access Issues

The University of Washington Center for Human Rights report (October 2025) revealed:
- **U.S. Border Patrol** and federal immigration agencies accessed Washington state Flock systems
- **At least 8 WA law enforcement agencies** enabled direct 1:1 sharing with Border Patrol
- **31 Washington jurisdictions** had their data searched by Border Patrol (thousands of searches)
- **Potential violation** of Washington's Keep Washington Working Act (2019), which restricts using state/local resources for civil immigration enforcement

### Community Response

- **Strong community advocacy** for removal led to suspension
- **Data privacy concerns** were primary driver
- **Immigration enforcement access** heightened sanctuary city concerns
- Olympia joins **growing list of cities** halting Flock programs (including Redmond, WA)

### Current Status

- **Program suspended** as of December 2025
- Cameras to be **removed**
- City reassessing surveillance technology policies
- No timeline announced for potential future surveillance programs

## 2. WASHINGTON STATE LEGISLATION (2026 Session)

The 2026 Regular Session of the Washington State Legislature (currently underway, Day 12 as of January 23, 2026) includes multiple bills addressing surveillance technology and AI-backed surveillance.

### A. Flock License Plate Camera Regulation

**Sponsor**: Senator Yasmin Trudeau (D-Tacoma)

**Purpose**: Create statewide regulations for automated license plate reader (ALPR) systems, including Flock Safety cameras

**Key Provisions**:
- Specific guidelines for accessing and using Flock camera data
- Balance between law enforcement tools and community surveillance concerns
- Ensure data use aligns with Washington state values
- Address federal access to locally-collected surveillance data

**Status**: Introduced in 2026 session

**Context**: Washington currently has **no law regulating ALPR technology**, though some other states have enacted related laws.

### B. AI Surveillance in K-12 Schools

**Bill Number**: Senate Bill 5956

**Purpose**: Ban AI-backed surveillance and predictive analytics in schools

**Key Prohibitions**:

1. **Predictive "Risk Scores"**
   - Bans systems that label students as likely troublemakers
   - Prohibits predictive algorithms for behavioral assessment

2. **Real-Time Biometric Surveillance**
   - Prohibits facial recognition systems
   - Bans real-time biometric identification in schools

3. **Automated Discipline Decisions**
   - Schools cannot use AI as sole basis for:
     - Suspensions
     - Expulsions
     - Referrals to law enforcement
   - Prohibits discipline based solely on AI predictions or scores

**Status**: Under consideration in 2026 session

**Impact**: Would make Washington one of the first states to ban AI surveillance in educational settings

### C. High-Risk AI Systems Regulation

**Bill Number**: House Bill 2157

**Purpose**: Regulate "high-risk" AI systems used in consequential decisions

**Key Provisions**:
- Require companies to protect people from **algorithmic discrimination**
- Address AI use in:
  - Hiring decisions
  - Housing applications
  - Lending and credit decisions
  - Law enforcement applications

**Status**: Introduced in 2026 session

### D. AI-Generated Content Disclosure

**Bill Number**: House Bill 1170

**Purpose**: Require disclosure of AI-generated media

**Key Provisions**:
- Mandate disclosures on AI-generated:
  - Images
  - Videos
  - Audio content
- Combat deepfakes and misinformation
- Enhance transparency in AI-generated content

**Status**: Introduced in 2026 session

### E. Surveillance-Based Price Discrimination

**Bill Number**: House Bill 2481

**Purpose**: Prohibit surveillance-based price discrimination and surge pricing

**Key Provisions**:
- Ban using surveillance data for personalized pricing
- Prohibit surge pricing for retail goods based on surveillance data
- Protect consumer privacy in commercial settings

**Status**: Introduced in 2026 session

### F. AI Companion Chatbot Safety

**Bill Numbers**: House Bill 2225 / Senate Bill 5984 (companion bills)

**Purpose**: Implement safeguards for minors interacting with AI chatbots

**Key Provisions**:
- Basic safety protections for children and teens
- Regulate emotionally responsive AI products
- Address mental health concerns with AI companions

**Status**: Under consideration in 2026 session

## 3. WASHINGTON STATE AI TASK FORCE RECOMMENDATIONS

### Report Released: December 1, 2025

The Washington State Artificial Intelligence Task Force released its interim report with policy recommendations for 2026.

### Key Surveillance-Related Recommendations

**Law Enforcement AI Use**:
1. **Public Disclosure Requirement**
   - Law enforcement agencies must publicly disclose AI technology use
   - Transparency about which AI systems are deployed
   - Information about how AI is used in policing

2. **Officer Attestation**
   - Officers must attest to accuracy of AI-assisted reports
   - Accountability for AI-generated information
   - Human oversight of AI recommendations

3. **Federal Regulatory Gap**
   - Task Force noted federal government's "hands-off approach" has created "crucial regulatory gap"
   - States must experiment with their own guardrails
   - Washington taking leadership role in AI regulation

**Final Report Due**: July 1, 2026

## 4. STATEWIDE SURVEILLANCE LANDSCAPE

### Current Situation

**Flock Safety Adoption**:
- Multiple Washington cities adopted Flock cameras in 2024-2025
- Growing backlash due to:
  - Federal immigration enforcement access
  - Privacy concerns
  - Lack of community consultation
  - Data retention and sharing practices

**Cities Suspending/Removing Flock Cameras**:
- **Olympia**: Suspended December 2025
- **Redmond**: Halted program amid privacy concerns
- **Growing movement** for "de-Flock victories"

**Cities Still Using Flock Cameras**:
- Multiple jurisdictions continue operations
- Facing increasing public pressure for transparency

### Legal Context

**Rodriguez v. City of Sedro Woolley** (Skagit County Superior Court, November 2025)
- **Landmark ruling**: Surveillance camera data must be disclosed under Public Records Act (RCW 42.56)
- Vehicle images automatically collected are **public records**
- Public access **regardless of whether used in specific investigations**
- **Applies statewide** as legal precedent

**Keep Washington Working Act** (2019)
- Restricts law enforcement from using state/local resources for civil immigration enforcement
- **Potentially violated** by federal Border Patrol access to Flock systems
- Legal challenges may emerge from UW report findings

**Current Regulatory Status**:
- **No statewide ALPR regulation** exists (as of January 2026)
- **Patchwork of local policies** across jurisdictions
- **2026 legislation** aims to create uniform standards

## 5. UNIVERSITY OF WASHINGTON CENTER FOR HUMAN RIGHTS FINDINGS

### Report: "Leaving the Door Wide Open" (October 2025)

**Key Findings**:

1. **Unauthorized Federal Access**
   - U.S. Border Patrol accessed local Flock Safety systems
   - Other federal immigration agencies also had access
   - Access may violate Washington state law

2. **Scope of Access**
   - **At least 8 WA agencies** enabled direct 1:1 sharing with Border Patrol
   - **31 Washington jurisdictions** had data searched by federal agents
   - **Thousands of searches** conducted
   - Border Patrol ran searches without local agency knowledge or consent in some cases

3. **Legal Implications**
   - Potential violation of Keep Washington Working Act
   - Threatens sanctuary city policies
   - Raises constitutional concerns about warrantless surveillance

4. **Lack of Oversight**
   - Many local agencies unaware of extent of federal access
   - No audit trails in some cases
   - Insufficient data sharing policies
   - Weak contractual protections against federal access

### Impact

This report catalyzed:
- Olympia's decision to suspend Flock program
- Increased legislative attention to ALPR regulation
- Community organizing against surveillance expansion
- Calls for stronger data privacy protections

## 6. BIPARTISAN MOMENTUM FOR SURVEILLANCE REGULATION

### Growing Coalition

**Support for Regulation Includes**:
- **Civil liberties organizations**: ACLU of Washington
- **Immigration advocacy groups**: Concerned about federal access
- **Privacy advocates**: Pushing for stronger data protections
- **Community groups**: Grassroots organizing
- **Some law enforcement**: Recognizing need for clear guidelines
- **Bipartisan legislators**: Democrats and some Republicans support regulation

### Key Concerns Driving Support

1. **Federal Immigration Enforcement**
   - Protecting sanctuary city policies
   - Preventing warrantless federal access
   - Safeguarding immigrant communities

2. **Data Privacy**
   - Mass collection of location data
   - Retention periods (30 days to indefinite)
   - Third-party data sharing
   - Lack of transparency

3. **Civil Liberties**
   - Warrantless surveillance
   - Chilling effects on First Amendment activities
   - Disproportionate impact on communities of color
   - Mission creep concerns

4. **Lack of Oversight**
   - No statewide standards
   - Inconsistent local policies
   - Limited community input
   - Minimal transparency requirements
